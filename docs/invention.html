The Invention of Internet

Learn about how and why the internet, the invention of the century, was created.


Emilia Ton

Dec 18 2020 - 4 min read

1. The Sputnik Scare

On October 4th, 1957 the Soviet Union launched Sputnik, a satellite which relayed blips and bleeps from its radio transmitters as it circled the Earth. To catch up and hopefully surpass the Soviets in space technology, President Eisenhower created the bureau at the Defense Department called ARPA the Advanced Research Projects Agency. It was in charge of the space program which then included computer science. Computer communication was in the embryonic stage but it was making progress. At MIT a graduate student named Leonard Kleinrock began applying queueing theory to data transmission.

2. ARPAnet

The seminal idea of the internet was envisioned by a psychologist, scientist and professor at MIT named Joseph Carl Robnett Licklider. In the 1950s, Licklider’s vision was that connecting all of the computers together would allow you to communicate and share information better. Computer communication was a flight of imagination far beyond anything at the movies. In the 1960s, America needed an indestructible communications network in the preparation of the Cold War. Paul Baran was hired to see if one could be built.

In 1966, Bob Taylor of ARPA needed a separate terminal to log into each of several time shared computers at research centers across the country. Taylor recruited Larry Roberts for the job. Roberts had built the world's first experimental connection between two computers at the MIT Lincoln Laboratory in Lexington, Massachusetts. 1968 also was the year Larry Roberts sought out the engineers to build the Advanced Research Projects Agency’s computer network called the ARPANET. It was going to be based on special-purpose mini computers called Interface Message Processors or IMPs and packet switching. On September 1st 1969 the first piece of hardware created for the computer network that was the forerunner of the internet arrived at UCLA. by the end of 1969 all four sites were interconnected. In April 1971, the ARPANET had 18 mainframe computers hooked into the network.

3. The invention of electrical mailing application

At first, there wasn't much to do on the ARPANET beyond transferring data files among its members but then a new capability was added the first killer application that would bring in the masses and take the usefulness of computer networking to a new level that killer app was email. Raymond Tomlinson was working on a mail program but it was programmed for a single computer so that you can send emails to yourself or other users on the sharing computer. He realized that the mailbox he was sending these emails to was a file and tried to send that file across the network instead of keeping it within the same machine. He modified the code and the first email program was created.

4. The TCP/IP Protocol

In 1973, Vinton Cerf and Bob Kahn teamed up to develop a set of rules or procedures known as the TCP/IP protocol which if followed by different computer networks would allow them to pass their message packets back and forth. The TCP/IP protocol enabled the networks to interconnect. Cerf and Kahn called the computer in the network that would know the protocol and thus be able to communicate across networks the Gateway. In 1983, after ten years of refinement and bureaucratic haggling TCP/IP was adopted as the universal standard marking a milestone in the development of the Internet, a common network of interconnected networks.

5. The World Wide Web

In 1991, the internet changed again. That year, a computer programmer in Switzerland named Tim Berners-Lee introduced the World Wide Web: an internet that was not simply a way to send files from one place to another but was itself a “web” of information that anyone on the Internet could retrieve. Berners-Lee created the Internet that we know today.

In 1993, a group of students and researchers at the University of Illinois developed a sophisticated browser that they called Mosaic. (It later became Netscape.) Mosaic offered a user-friendly way to search the Web: It allowed users to see words and pictures on the same page for the first time and to navigate using scrollbars and clickable links.
